Case Study: 1913 Dublin Strike and Lockout and Social Reform

Ireland Option 2

General

Ireland was skipped during the industrial revolution (18th - 20th century) but there were some factories in Ireland. Dublin was a small urban centre.

Socialism based on political and economic principle that says that all means of production should be owned by the state and shared equally among population - Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels.

Opposition to socialism in Ireland, why?

  1. Fear of revolution - socialism = revolution = collapse of society.
  2. Rural Ireland - majority = tenant farmers - did not apply, focused on industrial workers.
  3. Catholic Church - greatly opposed 'godless' ideal. Many socialists were very anti-clerical.
  4. Home Rule Party - socialism = threat to campaign.
  5. Unionist Party - made up of landowners + industrialists (wealthy class = anti-socialist).
  6. Sectarianism - difficult to unite Catholics and Protestants.

Karl Marx had campaigned for the release of Fenian prisoners in British jails. He knew they would support and provide a driving force for a worker's revolution.

trade union an organisation set up to represent the views and interests of workers - improved working conditions, better pay, shorter working hours, etc.

Trade unions in Ireland were for: skilled workers,had links with Britain and had moderate policies.

'New Unionism' unions representing unskilled workers - more radical (strike), leaders attracted to socialism.

Unskilled workers e.g. dock workers - worst payed, poor working conditions, lived in constant fear of unemployment, casual work.

Life in Dublin

majority of working class = unskilled workers.
Population growth was due to the expansion of government and trade, NOT manufacturing.

Housing
Tenement slums - severe overcrowding.
One family (+tenant) = one room.
Shared toilet(earth closet) facilities.
Unfit for human life, but rents were extremely high.
Most families were in debt and had to sell possessions to survive.

Health Issues
Infant mortality = high
TB and other water based diseases were widespread as drinking water ran alongside sewage.
Workers paid by day - bad food - expensive, small amounts.

Other Social Issues
Crime, alcoholism and prostitution were huge problems.

The working class were not interested in home rule, but they were not a political priority until the late 19th century when labour movements brought in reform

Labour Reform

  1. 1867 and 1884 - workers have the right to vote
  2. 1898 - Local Government Act - workers can vote
  3. Conservative rule - fear that they would limit trade unions (begin setting up own party)
  4. Frustration at Liberals slow reform
  5. Home Rule + Unionists were anti-trade unionist.

1899     11 trade unionists elected on Dublin Corporation
- failed to improve working conditions.

James Connolly

born: 1868, Edinburgh - Irish parents
age 14: sent to Ireland(army)
- married Lillie Reynolds(Protestant, Irish)
political view: republican, socialist.
  1896     relaunched Dublin Socialist Society
impact: merge socialism and nationalism.

The Irish Republican Socialist Party

launched: 1896 by Connolly and 7 activists
aims/ideas: minimum wage; max 48 hour week; nationalisation of banks; state control of railways; high taxes on wealthy; free education; pensions;
what did they do: Worker's Republic (1898)
              open-air meetings;
result: FAILURE
          party
          attacked by Church; Connolly moved to America until 1910;

James Larkin

born: 1846, Liverpool - Irish parents
political view: socialist
general: worked in Liverpool Docks - enormous energy but reluctant to compromise.
   1907   National Union of Dock Labourers send him to Belfast
result: success - unites Catholics and Protestants
   1908   Larkin moves to Dublin.

Irish Trade and General Workers Union (ITGWU)

established: December 1908
aim: unite workers into one powerful union
          better pay, working conditions - unskilled.
reputation: radical, socialist.
    1912   6 candidates on Dublin Corporation (including Larkin)
    1913   membership = 10,000
NO.1 enemy: William Martin Murphy.

William Martin Murphy
Wealthy employer, leading businessman, owned DUTC, Clery's, the Imperial Hotel and the Irish Independent.
Would not hire members of the ITGWU

DUTC (Dublin United Transport Company)
paid per hours worked, could be called anytime. Uniform insurance, and licence fees were deducted from wages, fined if tram was late, suspended without pay if passenger complained.

July - the Lock Out Begins

19th July - Murphy began replacing employees part of the ITGWU.
26 August 1913 - DUTC strike (horseshow week)
result: not huge impact - strike began morning and Murphy had most trams running within hours.
how: Murphy employed emergency crews and enlisted support of Dublin Metropolitan Police and RIC.
Larkin and leaders were arrested but released

31 August 1913 - 'Bloody Sunday'

ITGWU meeting organised, but banned.
    an alternative route was organised ( William O-Brien)
large crowd gathered on Sackville Street (Imperial Hotel)
-- Larkin(disguised as old man) appeared on balcony
-- immediately arrested, police turn on crowd
result: 2 killed, 400 injured (many civilians)
          deepened anger of strikers
          sympathetic strike
          more difficult for employers to find "scabs"/"black legs".

Sympathetic strike is a strike by workers not involved in the original dispute, to show their support for the workers on strike - refused to handle goods from that industry.

Employers UNITE!

3 September 1913 Murphy calls meeting of employers
- employers united = crush Larkin and ITWGU
sympathetic strike vs. sympathetic lock-out
Employers present workers with ultimatum: leave/ never join ITGWU or lose job.
Employers had financial advantage.
Police protected interests of employers.

September 1913 - over 20,000 workers were 'locked-out'

Aid for Workers

Families faced prospect of starvation.
Aid came from Catholic Church, general public, St. Vincent de Paul, British donated money and food('Kiddies Scheme'). Countess Markievicz - food kitchens.

End September 1913   board of enquiry set up to hear both cases.
workers: Larkin + Tim Healy (Home Rule MP)
compromise: employers withdraw ultimatum + re-hire
            union ends sympathetic strik + negotiate.
result: employers reject proposal :o
          public opinion on striker's side.

'Kiddies Scheme'

what: Irish Strikers' children sent to England
against: Catholic Church - English = Protestant
          published names of those who agreed to take part in the scheme in                 newspapers
result: FAILED

***Thing's Start to go Wrong***

end October 1913 Larkin jailed for seven months (Bloody Sunday) - released 2 weeks later.
Larkin heavily criticises British trade unionist leaders
result: Larkin condemned for his handling of the strike
          Murphy seeks help from British employers( Shipping Confederation)
                - free labourers - strike breakers

The Irish Citizen's Army (ICA)

established: 23 November 1913, James Connolly
aim: defend workers against employer oppression; protect workers from police atacks; restore morale in ITGWU.

January 1914 - Strike Ends

Morale was very low, families faced starvation - no choice.
Some employers allowed workers back without anti-union guarantee.
February - strike = fully over
ITGWU survived, led by Thomas Johnson.
Larkin leaves for America.
